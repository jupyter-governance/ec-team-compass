# Finances and grants

Project Jupyter's finances run through the Linux Foundation (LF).
This page explains how grants and other funds flow through the LF structures that hold Jupyter's money, so that anyone considering a grant on Jupyter's behalf knows where the money can land, how much overhead is taken out, and who controls how it is spent.
It is based on guidance from LF staff.

## How money is held

Two LF entities hold Jupyter funds, and they exist for different reasons:

- **The Jupyter Directed Fund** is the main pool of Jupyter money, governed by the foundation's governing board.
- **The Jupyter 501(c)(3)** is a separate nonprofit, controlled by the Jupyter Executive Council, a community-elected group that also sits on the governing board.

The 501(c)(3) is a "supporting organization" for the Directed Fund.
That legal relationship is what lets it receive funds and later move them into the Directed Fund.

:::{warning} Open question: grants raised by someone other than the EC
The Executive Council controls the 501(c)(3), so it directs how those funds are spent.
We have not settled what happens when someone else, such as a subproject or individual leader, raises a grant and expects to direct those funds themselves.
We need a clear policy for who decides how that money is spent.
:::

## Grants on behalf of a subproject

A grant "for Jupyter" can be received two ways:

- Into one of the LF entities above, so the money sits inside Jupyter's own structures.
- By an outside organization (for example, a university) that does the work and administers the grant directly.

Which route makes sense depends on who is best placed to execute the grant, and on where you want the money and its overhead to land (see below).

:::{warning} Open question: approval for grant work on Jupyter technology
We need a process for approving grant work that involves Jupyter technology.
A grant commits people to a particular vision and execution plan, and we want to be sure the project is aligned with both before anyone signs on.
Otherwise others can get legally committed to work that Jupyter isn't ready to accept.
:::

## G&A fees (overhead, indirect, etc)

The LF charges a general and administrative (G&A) fee on funds to cover the cost of administering them.
The fee is assessed when the money lands in its _final destination fund_, not when it first arrives.

- The rate is **9%** on the first $1M a project receives in a year, and **6%** on anything received above $1M that year.
- The 501(c)(3) on its own receives less than $1M per year, so money that _stays there_ is charged the **9%** rate.

The timing of the fee depends on where the funds will ultimately go.

Example: Money received into the 501(c)(3), but with the intent of moving it to the Directed Fund. When the money arrives, it is not charged G&A until after it transfers into the Directed Fund.
If the Directed Fund is already over the $1M threshold, funds moved in get the lower rate, even though they first arrived in the smaller 501(c)(3).
So routing money through the larger fund can reduce the overhead taken out of a grant.

## Restricted funds and how transfers are tracked

There is a tradeoff between moving money into the Directed Fund for the lower overhead and keeping it separately controlled in the 501(c)(3).

When the 501(c)(3) transfers money into the Directed Fund, it goes in without conditions.
This is deliberate: attaching conditions would make the money "restricted funding" under IRS rules and trigger extra reporting requirements.
The cost of avoiding that is that the money becomes part of the Directed Fund's single pot rather than a separately tracked bucket.

The Directed Fund's governing board then allocates that money where it best fits.
It can steer funds toward the priorities the Executive Council has laid out, so the EC's intent can still be honored in practice.
What it cannot do is make that an explicit written commitment tied to the transfer, since that would be the kind of condition that creates restricted funding.

If keeping funds under the Executive Council's direct control matters more than the lower overhead, the money can instead stay in the 501(c)(3).
It has its own budget and governance, but funds there pay the higher 9% G&A rate.
